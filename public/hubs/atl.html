Delay Patterns at ATL

Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport's Front Range location makes it vulnerable to dramatic and fast-moving weather systems. Understanding typical disruption patterns helps set expectations:

Winter (Nov‚ÄìMar)

Blizzards are the defining delay event at ATL. Major snowstorms can strand thousands of passengers and shut down operations for 12‚Äì24 hours. Atlanta's position on the plains means storms arrive fast with little warning. De-icing operations are extensive, and runway clearing after heavy snow takes time across ATL's six runways.

Summer (Jun‚ÄìAug)

Afternoon thunderstorms along the Front Range build almost daily, producing lightning, hail, and microbursts. These typically develop between 2‚Äì7 PM and can trigger ground stops. Morning flights have significantly better on-time performance.

Spring (Mar‚ÄìMay)

Late-season snowstorms are common and often the heaviest of the year. March and April blizzards regularly disrupt ATL operations. Wind events can also reduce runway capacity.

Tip: Morning departures (before 11 AM) consistently have the best on-time performance at ATL. Summer thunderstorms and winter storms both tend to peak in the afternoon and evening.
