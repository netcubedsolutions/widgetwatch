Hub Overview

Los Angeles International Airport is United Airlines' focus city and Pacific gateway, connecting Southern California and the entertainment industry to United's global network. With approximately 200 daily departures, LAX is not a full hub but plays a critical role in United's West Coast strategy.

United operates from Terminals 7 and 8 at LAX. Terminal 7 handles most domestic flights with a United Club lounge, while Terminal 8 serves additional domestic and select international routes. United Express regional partners operate from both terminals. LAX is undergoing a massive modernization including the Automated People Mover connecting all terminals.

Delay Patterns at LAX

Los Angeles International benefits from Southern California's generally mild weather, but several factors can still cause delays:

Marine Layer / June Gloom (May‚ÄìJun)

Coastal fog and low clouds ‚Äî locally known as "June Gloom" ‚Äî reduce visibility and can force instrument approaches, reducing arrival rates. Morning fog typically burns off by midday, making afternoon flights more reliable during fog season.

Santa Ana Winds (Oct‚ÄìMar)

Hot, dry offshore winds can reach 50+ mph in extreme events, causing turbulence on approach and occasionally triggering runway configuration changes. Severe Santa Ana events are rare but can significantly disrupt operations.

Volume and Ground Congestion

LAX is one of the busiest airports in the world by passenger volume. Taxi times can be lengthy, and the U-shaped terminal layout creates surface congestion. Terminal construction related to the Automated People Mover and other modernization projects may add ground delays through 2028.

Tip: LAX generally has good on-time performance compared to East Coast airports. Morning fog during May‚ÄìJune is the main weather concern. Early afternoon departures typically have the best reliability.
